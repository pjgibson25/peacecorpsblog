Random Stuff

Here is my front room in my house. It's pretty large and is nicely decorated, something I inherited right when I arrived to site.

My House

One guy, big house – Having heard stories from my parents about living with other people, I expected to live with a host family the whole service. This is not the case and all volunteers have their own living space (some have duplexes). My house is very large and can accommodate guests. I enjoy my own “me time” every once in a while and luckily the large house doesn’t freak me out.

Amenities – I got them. I have electricity, running water (20% of the time), a gas stove, and a refrigerator! I also get 4G using the verizon-equivilant service provider of Tanzania. It’s good enough service that using data from my phone, a hotspot, my laptop, a HDMI chord, and a projector, I was able to stream a live premier league game and project it onto my wall for myself and a few teachers to watch. Nuts!

Food

Cooking by myself – I have enjoyed to cook independently. Many Tanzanian men are shocked that I can fend for myself in this way, but I’m still standing so I can’t be doing too bad of a job. There is an incredible Peace Corps cookbook (and an extra at my house) that I will be bringing back to the United States. It is full of great recipes and has expanded my mind.

Snickers – is my favorite candy in country. Every time I go into town, I buy a snickers bar, return home, put it in the refrigerator, and eat it the same night. Now, almost every time I see Vern, she gets me a snickers and it is both cute and awesome.

Kiti moto – which translates to “hot seat” is my favorite local food in country. It is fried pork and has an interesting backstory for its name-origin. Muslims do not eat pork, so if they eat kiti moto, they are in the hot seat. Most tanzanians love to hear me say that I like kiti moto, and I still don’t really know why.

Other local food – is also super good. Rice/Ugali and beans is the most common local dish. Some mamas in town make a mean rice and beans. Additionally, a Zanzibari soup called “urojo” gives kiti moto a running for my favorite local dish.

Stuff is cheap! – With a few exceptions like gasoline or electronics, most everything here is cheaper than in the United States. Especially food. For example, the flat price for a single avocado is equivalent to 22 cents. Managing my money has been pretty simple so far, and if I start to run out I can always live on a small amount of money at site for a few weekends. Spending time in Iringa Town or other large towns generally costs more.

Bargaining – is crucial. In addition to things being cheaper in general, you can often reduce the price by bargaining with the seller. A good rule of thumb is to cut their price in half and watch their reaction. Doesn’t always work, but it’s a fun way to learn the language and integrate. All of the locals bargain like it’s nothing.

Totally Random

Going with the flow – One huge rose/blessing is the newfound ability to go with the flow. Turns out you can make plans super last second, and life goes on! Certainly this isn’t a great approach to certain parts of life (like teaching), but with respect to traveling it’s a blast. You don’t always need a rigid plan, and sometimes it’s really beneficial to have a loose plan or no plan at all! How Peace Corps is that???

People like when you talk in Swahili – When I go to the market and speak in swahili, I can often get a “zawadi” or present for speaking in Swahili. One time when I was watching football at a bar with my counterpart, a stranger bought me 3 beers because he was so happy to hear me speaking the native language.

Voice Messages – So voice messages through Whatsapp are awesome. I get / receive a few each day, mostly from Vern and Lindsay. They always cheer me up and keep me connected to the people closest to me.
